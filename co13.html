11+ Comprehension (CO13)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The old flagpole platform behind the science block had been empty for years, so our physics teacher asked if we wanted to turn it into a weather station. Not a shiny tower with spinning cups and wires—at least, not at first. We would begin with instruments we could build, calibrate, and explain. If we could prove the data was reliable for a month, the head teacher would pay for a rain gauge with a tipping bucket and a proper mount for an anemometer. We started by drawing a site plan. Trees to the west could shelter the platform; the brick wall on the east might reflect heat in the morning. We agreed to place the thermometer in a shaded box called a Stevenson screen, painted white, with vents to let air pass through. For rainfall, we used a straight-sided cylinder set level on a paving slab. Wind direction would be marked by a simple vane cut from aluminium, balanced on a nail over a bearing scavenged from a broken fidget spinner. The school caretaker inspected our mounts and insisted we tighten every bolt. Calibration took longer than we expected. For temperature, we compared our thermometer with two digital kitchen probes in a sealed bag of ice water, then in room air. For rain, we poured measured jugs of water through the cylinder and checked that one millimetre on the ruler matched one litre per square metre in the maths teacher’s notes. We logged wind changes by standing at the four corners of the playground and calling directions over walkie‑talkies while the vane spun. Our records included time, cloud type, and any notes about shade or gusts. After two weeks, patterns appeared. Temperatures jumped at 8:40 a.m. when sun hit the east wall, then settled after the shadow of the roof crossed the platform. Afternoon breezes usually arrived before the final bell, strongest on days with tall, fast‑moving clouds. Rain fell twice: a steady eight millimetres overnight and a noisy burst of four millimetres in twenty minutes that left chalky puddles along the path. Our geography class compared the readings with the nearest airport’s report and found we were close, but slightly warmer on still, sunny mornings—likely due to the wall’s heat. We shared the graphs at assembly and asked for two additions: the tipping‑bucket gauge and a better mount for the anemometer above roof level. The head teacher agreed, on the condition that we also publish a “Reading Day” guide for younger classes. We wrote simple steps—check the screen is closed, read at eye level, empty the cylinder carefully—and added a space to note “something the instruments can’t show,” like the smell of wet leaves or the sound of the first drops under the eaves. On the first Monday with the new equipment installed, rain tapped the bucket in even clicks. We could watch the line rise in near‑real time, a reminder that numbers tell a story best when someone is listening.

11+ Comprehension (CO19)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our coastal science club wanted to learn whether the creatures in the tidal pools changed across the season. Low tide after school gave us forty minutes before water crept back. We marked three pools on the same stretch of rocks—upper, middle, and lower shore—and set a rule: observe without prying animals off their homes. We would count what we could see and note water temperature, wave splash, and shade. The first survey felt like a treasure hunt. The upper pool hosted limpets and tiny snails clinging to rough rock. The middle pool held beadlet anemones—red buttons that folded when touched by a dropper—and a shore crab under a ledge. The lower pool, closest to the sea, swayed with fronds of seaweed and flashed with small fish. We used square frames made from bamboo and string to sample the same area each week, placing them in identical positions by aligning knots with a chip on the rock. Numbers told patterns by week three. As days warmed, the upper pool grew greener with algae, and more snails grazed its surface. The middle pool’s anemones stayed in place, but we counted more juvenile crabs after calm days. The lower pool’s fish vanished in rough weather and returned after two still evenings. We recorded a spike in water temperature during a sunny low tide; the upper pool heated quickest because it was shallow and sheltered from waves. One change worried us. A bright, unfamiliar seaweed appeared in the middle pool—stringy, with air bladders that kept it afloat. We compared photos with a field guide and decided it was likely an invasive species sometimes washed in by storms. We didn’t remove it ourselves. We emailed the local coastal ranger with our pictures and grid references. She thanked us, visited at the next low tide, and pulled a handful to inspect, leaving most rock surfaces untouched to avoid tearing native species. By the last week of term, we presented a poster in the corridor: a simple map of the three pools, four line graphs showing counts, and photos from the same angle each week. We wrote a short note on how to watch without harm: keep feet steady, wet hands before touching nothing, and replace any moved stones gently. The ranger added a line about reporting unusual finds with clear photos. Our last photo showed a child crouched at the edge, pointing at a crab while the water returned—learning that the shore is a lesson that moves.

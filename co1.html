Passage

In the first month of the new school year, Class 6A received an unusual assignment: turn the forgotten greenhouse behind the gym into a “Community Science Garden.” The class monitor, John, divided everyone into teams. One group loosened the soil and pulled weeds. Another sketched ideas for a simple irrigation system. A third group walked around the neighborhood to ask what plants residents hoped to grow. In the dustiest corner of the greenhouse, they discovered a weathered notebook with the words “Observe, Connect, Share” on the cover. It had been left by a graduate twenty years ago and was filled with sprouting charts, hand-drawn temperature lines, and a few dried leaves taped to the pages. At first, most classmates only wanted the garden to look “pretty” so they could post photos online. But after reading the notebook, the class changed its goal: the garden should attract visitors and also answer questions. They chose a theme—“Where does water come from, and where does it go?”—and built clear plastic channels to show how water moved. They placed barrels to collect rainwater and set up flip-style signboards at each path so people could ask a question, flip the card, and uncover a clue. Amy, the sports captain, turned a broken basketball net into a climbing frame for vines. Quiet Lily took charge of observing insects and learned to organize her notes in tables so patterns would be easier to compare. On exhibition day, families, neighbors, and younger students came to explore. Some guests touched the cool beads of water on a leaf and learned the word “transpiration.” Others followed the channels to the end and were surprised to find the outlet leading to the small kitchen garden behind the cafeteria. The principal smiled and said the greenhouse was no longer just a decoration; it was a working “problem laboratory.” Before everyone left, John wrote the date on the last page of the old notebook and added a line: “We didn’t finish this; we’re passing it on to whoever needs it next.”

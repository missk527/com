11+ Comprehension (CO21)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The technology club wanted a project that smelled like success—literally. We set ourselves a “Solar Cooker Challenge” for the spring fair: bake biscuits using only sunlight. No mains electricity, no gas, no secret kettle. We studied three designs: a shiny cardboard box with a clear lid, a panel cooker shaped like a flower, and a deep tube lined with foil. Our science teacher insisted on two rules: measure temperatures properly, and agree a safe recipe with nothing that spoils if underdone. We built prototypes from pizza boxes, foil, and glass from old picture frames. A black baking tray acted as a heat sink; a cheap oven thermometer peeped through a hole in the lid. We tried three biscuit mixes with different sugar levels to see which browned best at moderate heat. On cloudy days, the cooker sulked at 60°C; in full sun with a light breeze, it nudged 90°C. The panel design caught morning rays; the box design held heat longer; the tube warmed quickest but cooled fastest when wind picked up. The first batch tasted like warm dough with ambition. We adjusted: preheat for twenty minutes, insulate the box edges with pipe lagging, and tilt toward the sun at each break. A parent who repaired windows donated a roll of weather-strip to seal gaps. We logged data every ten minutes and drew tidy graphs. The panel cooker plus a darker tray gave the most even results. The tube did well if we shielded it from gusts with a cardboard screen. On fair day, the field looked like a garden of silver flowers. We set up a sign: “Solar biscuits—free sample when the thermometer reads 85°C.” Visitors asked whether it was worth the bother. We didn’t claim to replace ovens. We said that demonstrating heat capture helped people picture roofs full of panels, and that low-power cooking might matter in an outage. By mid-afternoon our best tray reached the target, and the biscuits tasted like sunlight meeting patience. The final poster showed a simple rule of thumb: shiny to reflect, black to absorb, clear to trap—and someone to mind the angle.

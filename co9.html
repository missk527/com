11+ Comprehension (CO9)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The old storage room beside the theatre hadn’t been used in years, unless you counted dust as a tenant. In January, our media club convinced the head teacher to let us turn it into a tiny community radio studio. We didn’t ask for expensive equipment. We asked for permission, a lock that worked, and a promise that if we met two goals—clear sound and useful programmes—we could stay on air. The plan was to stream online at lunch and one evening a week so neighbours could listen too. We scrounged parts from the school’s cupboard: a pair of battered microphones, foam from a craft box for makeshift filters, and a rolling cart that became a mixing desk. A parent who fixed bicycles donated a set of padded headphones. Another parent, a nurse, reminded us to sanitise gear between shows. Our physics teacher helped us understand why sound bounces in a small room; we built panels from old curtains and cardboard to dampen echoes. When we tested our first recording, the result was thin and buzzy. We laughed, then made a list: shorten cables, move mics closer, add a carpet square. By the third week, the sound improved enough that we planned real segments. “Morning Noticeboard” would summarise sports fixtures and club times. “Two Streets Over” would interview one neighbour each week about jobs people rarely see in films: bus scheduler, florist, recycling driver. We created a rule book: no rumours, name sources, ask permission before recording, and avoid music we didn’t have rights to. Our art club designed a tiny logo—a radio with a speech-bubble antenna—and our computing group set up an online player with a big pause button because not everyone likes autoplay. Going live felt different from rehearsals. Your own voice in the headphones sounded like a stranger. Our first guest was the librarian from the community centre, who explained how to request large-print books and home delivery. She also described how volunteers read stories over the phone to people who couldn’t leave the house. Listeners sent short messages through a form on our website. Some simply said “hello.” One asked when we would add traffic updates. Another wrote that the pause button was a relief. At the end of the first month, we reviewed numbers and notes. The audience was small but steady, with a bump on Thursdays after the neighbour interview. We made a simple chart for the head teacher and promised the same two goals for the next month: clear sound, useful programmes. He nodded and asked for one more: teach three Year 7 students how to run the desk. “If a station can train new voices,” he said, “it has a future.” We added a final line to our rule book: “Leave a guide for the next presenter.” Then we hit “record,” because the first lesson any station learns is that silence, too, is a choice.

11+ Comprehension (CO4)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Across the river from the old stadium, a grocery chain turned the roof of its largest store into a small apiary—four wooden hives painted blue, yellow, and white. On weekends, a volunteer group invites visitors to climb the stairs and learn why bees matter to cities. The project began after a hot summer when gardens produced fewer tomatoes and strawberries than usual. A biology teacher, Ms Patel, told the store manager that pollinators were likely struggling, and offered to help set up a pilot site if the company promised to keep records and share them with local schools. Some customers worried about stings and sticky honey leaking through the ceiling. The team responded with facts and design. The hives were placed far from the roof edge and surrounded by planters filled with lavender and thyme to give bees a reason to circle close. A mesh windbreak slowed gusts so the insects could land more gently. Clear signs explained that honeybees are usually calm when left alone and that foraging workers focus on flowers, not people’s lunches. The store also bought light suits and veils for training sessions, which quickly sold out because parents wanted a science activity they could do with their children. Keeping bees on a roof is not as simple as leaving boxes in the sun. The volunteers record weekly notes: queen-right or not, brood pattern, mite counts, and nectar sources. In late summer they weigh each hive to make sure there is enough food for winter. If a colony seems weak, they combine it with a stronger one. When autumn arrives, they reduce entrances so wasps and cold drafts are less of a problem. Data from the roof is compared with notes from parks and backyard keepers to spot patterns across the neighbourhood. The honey yield is modest—only enough jars to stock two shelves—but the impact reaches further. Students map which streets bloom earliest and suggest where to plant drought-tolerant flowers. The store hosts a “pollinator aisle” each May, setting out seed packets beside fruit and explaining how habitat supports harvests. The city council has started to include green roofs in new building guidelines, and a nearby hospital planted a small meadow along its ambulance road. On quiet mornings, you can stand by the windbreak and hear a sound that resembles a steady violin note. It is the hum of thousands of wings, stitching together gardens one visit at a time. The apiary doesn’t solve every problem facing insects, but it gives people a way to notice and to help—and noticing, the volunteers like to say, is where stewardship begins.

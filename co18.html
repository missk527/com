11+ Comprehension (CO18)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our library’s smallest room had once stored maps. Now the shelves were mostly empty, so we asked to start a “Seed Library.” The idea wasn’t to sell anything. It was to help neighbours borrow packets of locally adapted seeds in spring and return a small envelope of saved seeds in autumn. We began with easy vegetables—beans, peas, and lettuce—and a few flowers that welcomed pollinators. The librarian agreed if we could label everything clearly and never confuse saved seeds with snacks at events. We met on Saturdays to clean jars, fold paper envelopes, and write labels. Each packet carried four lines: variety name, planting window, days to harvest, and a space for the borrower’s notes. We added a “seed story” tag where people could write where the parent plants grew best—sun behind the shed, or pots on a windy balcony. A gardener from the allotments taught us to do a simple “float test” for older seeds: if many floated, germination might be low, so we should double the sowing. The first month taught us what we missed. The bean packets vanished in two days, but hardly anyone took the lettuce. We realised our display was all green on green. We swapped to bright dividers and put a photo of a salad bowl on the lettuce box. Borrowers began returning with questions: how deep is “shallow”? What counts as “moist but not soggy”? We made a quick reference card with drawings of finger depths and soil textures. We also added a “first timer” shelf with starter kits: six labelled envelopes, a pencil, and instructions on how to save seeds from beans without drying them on a radiator. By midsummer, the seed room felt like a club that didn’t require membership. People left notes: a child’s drawing of the first pea flower; a warning that pigeons found the sunflowers tasty; a tip to plant beans after the last cold snap even if the calendar said April. We tracked borrowing in a spreadsheet and noticed a pattern: gardeners in flats preferred compact varieties; those with gardens took climbers and corn. The council’s community garden asked whether we could add herbs next year, and the librarian nodded at our careful labels. “Yes,” she said, “as long as the stories travel with the seeds.”

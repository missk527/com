11+ Comprehension (CO17)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our island’s little ferry could carry twelve cars and forty foot passengers. On summer Saturdays, the queue snaked past the bakery by 9 a.m., and tempers rose with the temperature. The council wanted a bigger boat one day; our maths club suggested a smaller idea now: redesign the timetable and loading order using observations from two weekends. We counted arrivals in ten-minute blocks, recorded the mix of cars, bikes, and walkers, and timed how long the ramp stayed idle between sailings. We drew chalk lines to mark where the twelfth car should stop so late arrivals didn’t block the bakery door. We also tested two loading sequences under the crew’s supervision: cars first then walkers, and walkers first with bikes alongside. A parent who ran the café offered tokens for anyone willing to try the early 8:20 sailing. The numbers told a clear story. Demand peaked between 9:10 and 10:00, but the ramp sat empty for three minutes after several unloads. When walkers boarded first, cars moved in one smooth line with fewer handbrake pauses. Bikes loaded with walkers reduced wobbling near the ramp. The chalk line cut arguments by giving latecomers a visible rule. The tokens nudged twenty people to take the 8:20 boat, which returned before the rush with space to spare. We sketched a new timetable with two early sailings fifteen minutes closer together, then a steady half-hour rhythm. We proposed a loud, simple board at the queue: “Next departures: 8:20, 8:35, 9:05…; Cars stop at the second cone; Walkers line left; Bikes with walkers.” We also asked the council to try a “walk-on express” at 9:20 on the busiest days, with a staffer counting forty people quickly while the deck crew prepped cars for the next run. At the trial weekend, tensions softened. The early pair soaked up the first arrivals; the express lifted the heaviest slice of foot traffic; the chalk line and cone made late arrivals shrug instead of shout. By noon, the bakery still sold out of buns, but the queue no longer blocked the door. We didn’t claim to have solved summer. We presented a one-page summary: fewer idle minutes, fewer stops on the ramp, and the same total capacity spread more evenly. The harbour master circled one sentence and smiled: “Small changes, timed right, can feel like a bigger boat.”

11+ Comprehension (CO22)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our student newspaper wanted to run a story on a rumour that school lunches would be replaced by vending machines. Instead of publishing guesses, the editors announced a “fact-check week.” We listed claims on a whiteboard and divided tasks: interview the head teacher, read the council’s catering contract, and survey students about what they actually wanted from lunch. The rule was simple: cite sources, and colour-code confidence—green for confirmed, amber for uncertain, red for false or misleading. Interviews deflated the rumour quickly. The head teacher said the only change under discussion was a pilot for pre-ordering salads to reduce queues. The council confirmed that hot meals would continue under the existing contract for two more years. But the survey surprised us: many students wanted a faster line for simple items—soup, fruit cups, and bread—because they wasted time choosing. We also learned that students with clubs at lunch needed reliable timings more than new menu items. We checked our own bias. Two editors disliked the current curry and assumed others did too. The survey showed the opposite: curry days were popular if the spice level was marked clearly. We asked the kitchen for waste data and saw that unopened milk cartons remained a problem, echoing last term’s campaign. Our charts showed a small drop after the “take what you will drink” posters, but the carts still carried too many returns at the end of lunch. The final article didn’t shout. It began with a box titled “What’s true, what’s not.” It listed the rumour in red, the salad pre-order pilot in green, and two amber questions about timing and signage. Then it told a clearer story: long queues were a planning problem; the menu wasn’t vanishing; and small changes—like a separate quick line and clearer labels—might satisfy most students without drama. We added a promise to revisit the topic in a month with fresh data. Readers sent notes not about vending machines, but about soup flavours.

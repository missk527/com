11+ Comprehension (CO29)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

We wanted to count birds around the playing fields, but a drone would scare them and the council said no. So we made a “Sound Map”: at twelve marked points, students recorded one minute of audio on their phones at the same time each morning for two weeks. We clapped twice at the start of each clip so we could align recordings later, then stood still and faced the wind so our jackets wouldn’t scrape the microphones. Back in class, we labelled each clip with a simple code—A for blackbird, C for crow, R for robin, U for unknown—and kept a tally by location. We cross-checked by playing ambiguous clips to the music teacher and a local bird group volunteer. We didn’t try to identify every trill. The rule was “Be confident or say U.” Our map showed clusters: blackbirds preferred the hedges, robins favoured the fence by the allotments, and crows owned the tall floodlight poles. Rain made some days messy. We marked those squares grey and ignored them in totals. A delivery truck once idled by the gym and drowned out a minute at point 7, so we noted “noise event” and rescheduled an extra sample the next morning. By the end, our poster displayed a grid with small symbols for each species and a bar chart of counts per point. We suggested leaving the hedges untrimmed until fledglings had flown and moving the bins away from the floodlights so the crows would visit the field edge instead of perching over the walkway. At assembly, we played a rapid collage of calls so listeners could hear the difference between robin ticks and blackbird phrases. The head teacher asked if the project could repeat in winter. We said yes—and added that anyone could join by contributing one minute from the same points at the agreed time. The map would thicken with seasons, drawn not by drones but by ears.

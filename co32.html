11+ Comprehension (CO32)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The supermarket roof above our block of flats was a rectangle of gravel and vents. The manager agreed to let our eco club trial two beehives there if we wrote a care plan and kept the public entrance sealed at all times. We partnered with a local beekeeper who promised to visit weekly and to teach us in suits until we could help safely. We marked out a quiet corner away from air intakes and built wind shields from recycled pallets. We planted tubs with thyme and lavender and asked neighbours to share balcony herb cuttings. The beekeeper explained that urban bees can thrive if they have safe water and varied flowers, but that heat from roofs can stress hives in summer. We filled shallow trays with pebbles and topped them up so bees could drink without drowning. First inspections showed calm bees and healthy brood. We logged weather and the colour of pollen on returning legs—bright orange from marigolds, pale from clover. In July, a heatwave tested the hives. We rigged shade cloth and moved water trays to the cooler side of the shields. A security guard texted when he noticed wasps prowling; the beekeeper reduced the entrance size so the guard bees could manage it. By late August, we spun our first frames in a borrowed extractor and filled a dozen small jars. We labelled them “Rooftop—Not for Sale” and gave them to volunteers and the store’s night staff. Our poster in the foyer urged neighbours to keep pots flowering into autumn. The manager renewed the permission for another year, on condition we send a two-page report and keep the suits locked in the roof cupboard.

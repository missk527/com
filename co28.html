11+ Comprehension (CO28)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our eco garden had three compost bins that steamed on cold mornings. The science club wondered how much heat they made and whether turning them more often mattered. We stuck waterproof thermometers into the centre and near the edge of each pile, labelled A, B, and C. Bin A would be turned every week, Bin B every two weeks, and Bin C left alone as a control. We recorded outside air temperature and rain. For two weeks, the numbers crept up. The centre of Bin A reached 60°C by day ten; Bin B hit 55°C; Bin C sat around 45°C and smelled a little sour. We added shredded cardboard to balance the food scraps and measured again. Turning A released a warm, sweet smell and clouds of steam; the edge readings rose after mixing as heat spread outward. We noted a drop after heavy rain, which cooled the top layers and compacted the material. We built a graph and invited Year 7 to guess which line was which. Most guessed right after spotting that Bin A’s line climbed fast and dipped briefly on turning days, then recovered. We also tested a practical question: could the heat dry gloves on a grid placed over the vent holes? The answer was yes, slowly, but only on Bin A during peak heat. For safety we posted a sign: “No hands inside; use a fork to stir.” At the month’s end, we compared volumes. Bin A shrank the most and produced the driest, crumbliest compost. Bin B did well but slower; Bin C stayed lumpy. Our poster concluded: regular turning plus cardboard speeds composting and spreads heat for more consistent breakdown. We added a request to cooks: chop scraps smaller, avoid too much citrus, and cover scraps with brown material to keep smells down. The garden didn’t just eat waste; it warmed the air by the gate like a small, patient engine.

11+ Comprehension (CO16)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our history teacher loved exhibits that people could touch, so the school foyer became a “Pop-up Museum of Local Inventions” for one week. We didn’t have famous patents to display. We had everyday problem-solvers: a bricklayer’s line-holder from the 1960s, a midwife’s hand-written birth log, and a bicycle lamp that a grandfather rewired with parts from an old radio. The plan was to invite the inventors—or their families—to stand by each object at lunchtime and tell short stories. We created object labels with four fields: the problem, the idea, the first test, and the change after feedback. The bricklayer’s holder kept the line taut across uneven ground; the first version split in the rain until he rounded the corners and used a different wood. The birth log showed how the midwife mapped addresses to save time when bicycles were the fastest transport in town. The lamp’s label included a small circuit sketch and a photo of the original radio. Crowds surprised us on day one. Primary pupils pressed close to the lamp and asked why radio parts could power a light. We built a demo with a hand-cranked generator and a tiny bulb so they could try. A teenage visitor asked whether the bricklayer’s tool counted as an invention if it wasn’t patented. We added a note to the label: “An invention is a useful, new way of solving a problem—patents are one way to protect some ideas, not the only sign of invention.” We also taped a QR code to each stand that linked to a two-minute audio clip of the owner telling a memory about the object in their own voice. Feedback changed the layout midweek. People crowded the popular table; others were missed. We tested a timed loop: every ten minutes, a small bell rang, and presenters swapped places. This spread attention and gave voices a rest. We also placed a “design a fix” table with pencils and sticky notes. By Friday, the board was full of sketches—shoe grips for icy steps, a cup that clicks onto a book so you don’t spill tea, a fold-flat carrier for shopping on bikes. The foyer felt like a workshop that remembered as it invented. On the last afternoon, we made a booklet with photos, label texts, and one sentence from each owner: “What lesson would you pass on?” Many wrote about testing, listening, and changing. The head teacher asked if the booklet could become a yearly tradition. We said yes—on one condition: that each year’s pop-up includes room for new hands to try an old idea and for old hands to welcome new ones.

11+ Comprehension (CO11)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our town’s hall smelled like solder and cinnamon on the same Saturday—the first time the community ran a Repair Café beside the weekend bake sale. People arrived with lamps that blinked, zips that stuck, and toasters that had decided bread was a stranger. Our school’s design club volunteered to help greet visitors, log items, and match each person with a fixer. The rules were simple: repairs were free, advice was free, and replacements were up to you. If fixing wasn’t possible, we tried to help people decide whether to donate parts or recycle safely. Before the doors opened, mentors gave us a briefing. “Ask three questions,” said Malik, a radio technician. “When did it last work? What changed? What have you already tried?” Those questions, he said, were a map. Another volunteer rolled out a “triage table” with screwdrivers, zip ties, a multimeter, and a tangle of cables sorted into clear tubs. Beside it, a sewing teacher checked needles, spare buttons, and threads. On the wall, someone pinned a big sign: “We repair together. You keep your hands on your own item.” The first hour felt like a puzzle race. A kettle that refused to boil turned out to have a loose spade connector; we tightened it and wrote “check limescale monthly” on a small card for the owner’s fridge. A coat with a broken zip slider was mended with a five‑minute swap of a two‑pound part. A tablet that “wouldn’t charge” revived when we cleaned lint from its port with a toothpick and a burst of air. Not everything was quick. A turntable with a worn belt had to wait for a part, and a cracked phone screen went to a professional—sharp glass and home repairs don’t mix. At midday the organiser rang a bell so bakers and fixers would actually eat. While we chewed cinnamon rolls, we added tick marks on a poster: items repaired, items advised, items sent on. One visitor asked whether fixing old things was worth it if new ones were cheap. Malik shrugged and pointed at the line of people learning how to open a plug safely. “Skill is value, too,” he said. A parent signed up for next month’s event after watching her child replace a lamp switch with help from a volunteer. In the last hour, the hall grew quieter. Someone tuned a radio; the song sounded clearer than before, as if the room had been repaired too. We counted the day’s totals: thirty‑one items fixed, eleven advised, six referred to specialists, and a bucket of parts sorted for reuse. We recycled a shoebox of dead batteries and posted our numbers online with a list of the most common faults. The organiser thanked everyone and added one more line to the poster: “Bring a friend, and bring a question.” The repairs would continue next month, but the habit—ask, learn, mend—could start tonight at anyone’s kitchen table.

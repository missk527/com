11+ Comprehension (CO3)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

On the first weekend of spring, our town tested a handmade “tide clock” beside the harbour. It wasn’t a digital screen or a phone app. Instead, a round wooden face had a single hand that moved slowly to mark “high tide,” “falling,” “low tide,” and “rising.” The clock was part of a student project to help walkers, fishers, and kayakers plan their time safely along the shore. The idea came after two middle-school friends, Noor and Ethan, were caught on a narrow strip of sand when the water suddenly rushed back in. They made it to the steps in time, but the moment scared them enough to start asking questions. They learned that the sea in our bay rises and falls roughly twice every twenty-four hours, but not at the same times each day. The moon’s gravity and the coastline’s shape both matter. With a science teacher’s help, the pair collected a month of readings: when the water reached a certain marker on the pier, they wrote down the time. They also checked online tables from the national weather service to verify whether their notes made sense. The more they learned, the more they realized people needed a simple way to “read” the tide without looking up numbers. The first design was a poster with arrows showing safe and unsafe zones, but it sat flat and was easy to ignore. Later, Noor’s grandfather, a retired carpenter, offered scraps of cedar to build the clock body. Ethan designed the face on paper: four labelled arcs and small icons—a boot print, a fish, a kayak—placed where each group should be most careful. When the wood was ready, they installed a slow-turning mechanism powered by a cheap solar cell. The tide hand completes one full circle in about twelve hours and twenty-five minutes, then keeps going, day after day. On launch day, a small crowd gathered near the stairs to the water. A local reporter took photos while a councilor cut a ribbon. The science teacher reminded everyone that the clock was a guide, not a guarantee: storms and strong winds can make the water arrive earlier than expected. Still, the clock encouraged conversations. Children pointed at the icons and guessed who should wait before stepping onto the rocks. A pair of early-morning swimmers checked the hand and decided to use the pool that day. After a week, the harbour master agreed to keep the clock mounted through summer, as long as a volunteer group signed up to check it weekly. Noor and Ethan didn’t promise that their invention could predict everything, but they had made the tides easier to notice—and noticing, they said, is where safety begins.

11+ Comprehension (CO14)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our town liked being called “the stargazing valley,” but the name had become a story more than a truth. On clear nights, the football ground glowed like a small sun, and the supermarket’s car park shone as if noon could be switched on. For science week, our astronomy club ran a “Light Audit” to measure how much skyglow we had and what could be changed without turning the town dark. We borrowed meters that recorded sky brightness and simple lux readers for ground light. Pairs of students walked preset routes after homework on three evenings: one before moonrise, one with a half-moon, and one after a rain that washed the air clean. At each stop—library corner, riverside path, the hill behind school, and the football ground—we took two readings: up at the sky and down at the pavement. We also noted the lamp type: bare bulb, full shield, or decorative globe. A parent who worked as an electrician explained that shields direct light downward; globes throw it everywhere. Patterns appeared even before we made charts. The hill behind school had the darkest sky reading and the lowest ground light; walkers there used head torches and didn’t linger. The riverside path had bright, white globes that lit branches as well as benches, and moths circled them in glittering swarms. The supermarket car park used bright LEDs that never dimmed, even after midnight. Outside the football ground, lamps on tall poles spilled light beyond the fence; during practice, the beams reached the clouds like pale ladders. We tested small fixes. On the quietest evening, the club fitted cardboard “trial shields” to two globes on the riverside path with the council’s permission. The next reading showed the ground was just as visible, while the sky reading improved slightly. We tried a timer on a portable floodlight at the school steps; the meter rose darker when it clicked off at 9:30. We emailed a simple table to the council: site, lamp type, current reading, and “what-if” reading with our trial. We also added notes about safety: shields reduce glare and can actually help people see faces and steps more clearly. At the community meeting on Friday, not everyone agreed. A shopkeeper liked the bright car park for customers who arrived late. A runner said the riverside path felt safer with glow all around. Our club didn’t ask for blackouts. We proposed three changes: full shields for globes along the river, timers for low-traffic car-park areas after midnight, and angled fixtures at the football ground to keep beams on the pitch. The council agreed to test two of the ideas for a month. On our walk home, the clouds were thin and the first stars appeared early. We could still read the street signs, and we could finally count Orion’s belt without squinting.

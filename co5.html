11+ Comprehension (CO5)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

When our town’s central library announced a “Library of Things,” some people cheered and others frowned. The cheerful crowd imagined borrowing sewing machines, telescopes, and board games the way they borrowed novels. The frowning group asked whether a library should lend anything that wasn’t a book. The head librarian, Mr Dawson, explained that reading is still the heart of the library, but reading isn’t the only way people learn. He pointed to a shelf of repair manuals and said, “A manual is an invitation. The tool you need to try the instructions is the reply.” The pilot collection began with donations: a pair of binoculars, two ukuleles, and a toolbox with a missing screwdriver. Volunteers cleaned each item and added checklists so borrowers could confirm everything was present on return. A QR code on every case linked to short guides: how to tune a ukulele, how to find the moon’s craters, how to avoid over-tightening a screw. The most popular item in the first month wasn’t flashy. It was a simple cake tin shaped like a train; families borrowed it for birthdays, washed it carefully, and returned it with photos. Rules were necessary. Borrowers had to be over twelve or bring an adult. Items were loaned for one week and could be renewed if no one else was waiting. Some objects required a quick safety talk at the desk; for the sewing machine, a librarian demonstrated how to keep fingers away from the needle and how to use the emergency stop. When a lamp was returned with a cracked shade, the staff didn’t cancel the program. Instead, they added a small repair station: glue, spare screws, and a label maker for parts. As the months passed, the library collected data. Which items travelled most? Which sat quietly? They discovered that the telescope was busiest near the dates of meteor showers, while the tool kit peaked during school holidays. A set of noise-cancelling headphones rarely moved until exam season, when it disappeared and reappeared every week. The library posted a public list of requests: bike pumps, picnic blankets, a food dehydrator. Local businesses offered discounts on replacements when parts wore out. The town council asked whether the program saved residents money. Mr Dawson answered that saving money was helpful, but the deeper value was shared confidence. “Skills grow when people borrow the courage to try,” he said. Not everyone changed their mind, but more people joined the conversation. A retired engineer started a Saturday “fix-it table,” showing teenagers how to test batteries and strip wires safely. A parent who borrowed the ukulele ended up teaching chords to a small group after school. When a storm knocked out power one evening, the library’s camping lanterns—previously ignored—were gone within an hour. In the morning light, borrowers brought them back with thanks and stories. The Library of Things didn’t replace books; it offered another way to read the world—through doing, sharing, and returning.

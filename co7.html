11+ Comprehension (CO7)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

When three bike racks appeared outside our school, most of us treated them as decoration. Only a few students rode in, and they usually took the flattest route along River Way, even though buses idled near the depot there each morning. In September, our geography teacher proposed a project called “Clear Paths” to map safer, cleaner journeys to school. We would not campaign first and measure later. We would measure first. The equipment looked like pencil cases with straps: low-cost sensors that recorded nitrogen dioxide and fine particles every minute. Over two weeks, volunteer teams clipped the boxes to rucksacks and cycled different routes in pairs. We left at the same time each day so our data would be comparable. Our teacher asked us to write field notes—where we waited at traffic lights, where cars queued, where the smells changed from bread at the bakery to diesel near the depot. Back in class, we uploaded the numbers and turned them into lines on a map. The River Way path was fast but regularly spiked with pollution during bus warm‑ups. A longer route, up Elm Street and past the library, showed lower peaks except on rainy days when drivers crowded the hill. The surprise champion was a zigzag through side streets with speed bumps; it looked messy on the map but kept levels steady and low. Our science club borrowed a portable noise meter, and we added decibels to the map as a second layer. The side-street route was quieter too. We didn’t stop at dots and lines. We tried interventions. One group tested whether leaving ten minutes later avoided the depot spike, and the graph dipped exactly where we hoped. Another group asked the bakery if we could place a poster by the door showing the library route; on the first Friday, six new riders followed it. We wrote a short report for the city council proposing two things: a painted lane on Elm Street near the pinch point, and a “no idling” sign by the depot gates between 7:30 and 8:15. At assembly, some students complained the zigzag felt slower. “It is slower,” our map team admitted, “by three minutes on average.” But the next slide showed the three‑minute trade: steadier air and less noise. The head teacher agreed to place the bike racks closer to the side‑street entrance, and the bus company manager visited to discuss a trial of later warm‑ups. We didn’t declare victory. We said that noticing, testing, and changing routes had already turned the racks from decoration into an invitation—and that invitations work best when someone answers them.

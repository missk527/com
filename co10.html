11+ Comprehension (CO10)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

When two spring storms flooded the corner by Maple and 3rd, the council promised bigger drains. Our science club proposed something smaller first: a rain garden on the school’s front lawn to catch run‑off from the roof. Some parents imagined a muddy hole. The design looked nothing like a pond. It was a shallow basin with native grasses and a stone-filled trench called a swale leading from the downspout. The idea was to slow water, sink it into the soil, and filter grit before it reached the street. We began by mapping where puddles formed after rain. Using chalk, we traced the edges, then measured how long the water stayed. A geography teacher showed us how to do a percolation test: dig a narrow pit, fill it with water, and time the drop. Our soil drained steadily but not fast; we would need a mix of sand and compost under the planting layer. The caretaker marked utilities, and we phoned a local nursery that donated tough plants—switchgrass, sedges, and purple coneflowers. Installation day felt like a festival. Volunteers lifted turf, shaped a shallow bowl, and lined the swale with river stones. A small weir—two boards between stakes—created a step so the basin wouldn’t overflow onto the path. We set a ruler in the center marked “0–15 cm” to read depth during storms. A pair of younger pupils painted a sign that explained how the garden worked and reminded dogs to keep out while roots settled. Before we finished, a light drizzle arrived and trickled along the stones exactly as drawn on the plan. The first big test came a week later. An afternoon downpour hammered the roof and filled the gutter like a drum. Instead of sheet water racing over the pavement, the swale shone silver and the basin rose to 9 cm, then sank to 3 cm by evening. The next morning, except for a damp ring, the lawn looked ordinary. We posted a graph on the school website showing depth change over time and invited families to add observations from their doorsteps: Were nearby drains less clogged? Did puddles shrink? Not every comment was praise. One neighbour worried about mosquitoes. We explained that rain gardens are designed to empty within two days—too quick for most mosquitoes to hatch—and added a note to the sign. A parent asked whether the garden could freeze in winter and push stones onto the path. We built a small wooden lip along the edge as a shield. Two months later, the council still planned its bigger drains, but our corner flooded less often. The rain garden hadn’t solved the town’s storms; it had made one place ready to welcome the water that always comes.

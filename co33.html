11+ Comprehension (CO33)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our town had forecasts from TV and a distant airport, but the hills around us twisted weather in ways maps didn’t show. We built a simple community weather app for phones that let people post observations—rain starting, fog at the river, frost on the hill—and see them on a live map for the next two hours. The rules were polite: no chatter, no names; only short weather notes with time and place stamped automatically. We tested four notification types: rain ping, frost alert, wind gust, and “clear now.” Dog walkers loved “clear now” for choosing a quick loop. Cyclists appreciated gust alerts at the ridge. To check quality, we compared posts to school instruments and the public station by the library. False reports were rare and faded quickly because each note expired after two hours unless others nearby confirmed it. A baker suggested adding road surface notes at dawn—“black ice by the bridge”—and the council asked for a “flood gate open” marker they could tap during storms. We added icons and a simple thumbs-up to confirm. On the foggy Thursday before the market, the map showed a ribbon of low cloud along the river while the hilltop remained sunny. The head teacher used it to delay the coach departure by fifteen minutes; visibility improved and the trip left safely. By the end of term, we published a one-page guide: what to post, what not to post, and how to set quiet hours. The app didn’t replace forecasts; it stitched small truths together so neighbours could plan. When the first snow came, the pings sounded like popcorn—and then, just as quickly, fell silent as the clouds moved on.

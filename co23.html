11+ Comprehension (CO23)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The new playground had two gates and one popular slide. At break, the queue tangled like spaghetti, and small children were squeezed by older ones turning back for a second go. Our design club taped chalked lines into a “line maze”: two lanes in, one lane out, and short bypasses to step aside if you changed your mind. We asked the head teacher to let us test it for three days with volunteer monitors wearing bright sashes. We measured three things: time from gate to slide, number of bumps reported by monitors, and how often children cut across lines. Day one felt strict but smooth; time fell by a third. Day two brought rain. The chalk blurred, and the out lane vanished; bumps rose again. We adjusted: thicker tape over chalk, cones at the gate, and a rule that after sliding you walked through an “exit arch” before rejoining. Day three worked best. Children learned the pattern and started helping each other: “Stay in your lane,” a tall Year 6 said, opening a bypass for a Reception pupil who had second thoughts. We posted results on the notice board with a simple map. The monitors recommended two permanent changes: paint the exit arch on the tarmac and add a sign with a cartoon of the maze so new pupils could read the rules without reading words. At the Friday assembly, the head asked if we wanted barriers. We said no—lines, signs, and habits beat fences if people understand them. We didn’t eliminate queue-jumping forever, but the maze reduced bumps and sulks. The slide was still fun; it was now fairer fun.

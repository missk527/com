11+ Comprehension (CO12)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

On the first day back after winter break, our biology teacher taped a note to the science block’s glass corridor: “If you find a bird near this window, tell us immediately.” Two sparrows had hit the glass in one week. No one wanted a corridor that looked like sky to birds but was actually a mirror. Our eco club proposed a small study and a fix. The head teacher agreed on two conditions: collect data for two weeks, then recommend a change we could install ourselves. We kept a simple log. Each morning and at lunch, two students walked the corridor and checked the planting beds outside. We noted weather, time, and whether lights were on. We also held a sheet of white card inside the glass and a dark cloth outside to see how reflections changed. On bright days, the corridor became a ribbon of sky; on dull ones, it turned into a tunnel lined with trees—the opposite of safe either way. Three soft taps and one hard strike happened in the first week. We phoned a local wildlife group, who explained that narrow stripes or dots on the outside of the glass could help birds “read” the window as a surface instead of open air. We tested two options using removable film. One panel got vertical stripes five centimetres apart; another got a grid of small dots five centimetres by five. A third panel, left clear, became our control. For three days we rotated which section was the control to avoid bias if birds favoured one side. We also measured inside visibility for students by asking, “Can you clearly see the oak tree’s trunk through this panel?” and rating yes or no. The stripes looked obvious up close but almost disappeared from inside class. The dots were subtle from every angle. During week two, no strikes were heard on the striped panel. A soft tap happened once on the dotted one. Two taps and a hard strike occurred on the clear control. We sent the numbers and a short chart to the head teacher and asked to install a full set of stripes across the problem corridor. The facilities manager allowed us to use a water-and-soap method to position the film and a rubber squeegee to smooth it. We borrowed ladders and worked in pairs on a Saturday, leaving a narrow gap at the bottom for drainage. By Monday morning, the corridor looked patterned from the playground but nearly normal from inside. A notice beside the door explained the stripes and thanked the wildlife group for advice. The eco club promised to keep the log going once a week for the rest of term. We didn’t claim a perfect solution. We wrote, “This design reduces collisions here; tell us if you observe an incident elsewhere.” If safety grows from attention, then the thin white lines were not decorations. They were a way of asking birds to notice—and a way of reminding us to notice back.

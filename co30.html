11+ Comprehension (CO30)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The clock in the old town hall had missed so many minutes that people used it as a joke. “Meet you when the clock decides,” they said. Our history club asked the council if we could research a restoration plan. The answer was cautious: we could document, not touch. We climbed the narrow stairs with a caretaker and found a dusty room with a cast-iron frame, two big weights on chains, and a pendulum the size of a canoe paddle. A pigeon had nested in one corner; a spider web joined the hour hand’s gear to a beam. We drew diagrams and wrote down what moved what. Winding the weights powered the gears; the escapement ticked the pendulum; rods ran to the faces. The caretaker said a specialist visited five years ago and oiled parts, but the clock still drifted and sometimes stopped. We logged temperature and drafts near the pendulum and noticed a cracked window that let wind rock the rod on stormy days. We timed the swing with a stopwatch: it wandered faster after gusts and slower as dust clogged the crutch—a forked arm that nudged the pendulum each tick. We interviewed shopkeepers who remembered when the chime matched the school bell, and we read a manual for a similar mechanism online. The manual warned against random oiling: too much oil attracts grit, making things worse. It recommended cleaning, replacing bushings, and sealing drafts near the pendulum. Our report suggested three steps: fit a clear cover around the pendulum path to calm air, repair the cracked window, and hire a clock restorer to inspect worn pivots. We also proposed a winding rota so the weights would never run low on busy weeks. At the council meeting, we showed a short video of the clock stopping as wind pushed through the gap. The councillors agreed to the window repair immediately and to seek quotes for the rest. The caretaker smiled at the rota suggestion and pinned a card by the stairs with initials for Monday and Thursday. A month later, the clock kept time well enough that jokes changed. “Meet you at the clock,” people said, and they did.

11+ Comprehension (CO6)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The first time the cafeteria weighed its rubbish, everyone was embarrassed. A clear bin showed exactly what students left on their trays: apple cores, unopened milk cartons, and mountains of rice. Our science club’s “Waste Week” was not meant to shame anyone, but to measure what really happened at lunch. We set up a long table with three labelled tubs—“Edible Leftovers,” “Compost,” and “Landfill”—and asked volunteers to help every day for five days. A teacher with a kitchen scale logged numbers on a tablet while we called out the weights. On Monday, the edible tub filled fastest. Many students said the portions of curry were too spicy or that they ran out of time to finish. We posted a big chart beside the exit showing totals for the day. Some people frowned at the height of the bars; others took photos. On Tuesday, the kitchen tried a milder sauce and added orange slices to the salad bar. That day, unopened milk still piled up. A Year 7 student admitted he took milk automatically because the cartons looked “free,” even though he never drank them. By Wednesday, patterns emerged. Compost stayed steady, mostly vegetable peels and napkins. Landfill dropped a little after we put a “liquids here” jug near the sink so cartons could be emptied before tossing. Edible leftovers were still the tallest bar, so we added a “Share Shelf” for sealed items. Students who didn’t want their yoghurt left it there; someone else could take it legally because the school’s policy allowed sealed food sharing within the same lunch period. On Thursday, the head cook stood beside our table and asked for comments. Students wrote quick notes on sticky paper: “More time to eat,” “Smaller rice scoop,” “Can we choose half portions?” The cook laughed at a dramatic drawing of a volcano labelled “Curry Level 5” and said she would adjust the spice level again. That afternoon, she emailed the principal asking for permission to offer half portions without extra payment. Friday brought our biggest change. The principal approved the half-portion option, and the kitchen moved the fruit to the start of the line. We measured a noticeable drop in edible leftovers and landfill. At the celebration assembly, our club didn’t say we had solved waste forever. We said that noticing, measuring, and responding had already made lunch fairer and cleaner. The principal promised to repeat Waste Week each term, and to invite families to a Saturday “leftover recipes” workshop. As we cleaned the scales and folded the charts, the bins looked less like evidence of failure and more like the beginning of a habit: weigh, learn, and try again.

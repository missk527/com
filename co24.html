11+ Comprehension (CO24)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The bus stop outside school had a bench but no shelter. On windy days, umbrellas became kites. Our product design class proposed a low-cost shelter we could build with community help. The council allowed a trial if we used recycled materials, kept sight lines clear for drivers, and posted a maintenance rota. We measured the space and noticed two constraints: a tree root that lifted the paving and a drain cover where puddles formed. We sketched three frames: a curved polycarbonate roof on timber posts; a flat roof with gutter into a barrel; and a modular frame made from steel scaffold offcuts with clear panels. We rated each for cost, wind resistance, and ease of assembly. The scaffold frame won—strong, bolt-together, and easy to adjust around the root. A local builder donated clips; the art department offered to sand the panels so they’d be clear but slightly frosted for privacy without blocking views. Build day felt like a barn raising. Teams sorted bolts, checked levels, and set rubber feet to protect the paving. We angled the roof to shed rain away from the queue and added a short gutter that fed the drain instead of the barrel to avoid mosquitoes. A small plaque listed the rota for sweeping leaves and checking bolts monthly. We also painted a thin yellow line to guide the bus’s stopping point and added a “mind the puddle” grate over the low spot. During the first week, students reported drier shoulders and fewer tangled umbrellas. One driver said the frosted panels reduced headlight glare. A neighbour worried the shelter might become a billboard for posters; we added a small cork board labelled “Community notices only” and set a monthly clean-off rule. At Friday assembly we shared the budget, the rota, and a request: if a bolt loosens, write the date on the plaque next to the maintenance tick-box and tell reception. The council extended the trial for the year, and the queue learned where to stand without blocking the path.

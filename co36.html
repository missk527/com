11+ Comprehension (CO36)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our town hall hosted a monthly Repair Café where volunteers fixed lamps, toasters, and torn jackets for free. The tables were always full, but the rota was chaos: three people soldered at once while no one stitched, or a crowd came with bicycles when the only mechanic was away. Our computing club offered to design a fair rota and a booking system that would not scare walk-ins. We started by interviewing volunteers about their skills and limits. One retired electrician preferred two short shifts; a student tailor could come only on alternate Saturdays; the bike mechanic loved mornings but had to leave by noon. We mapped likely demand from last year’s sign-in sheets and added a “surge buffer”—two flexible slots each hour for whatever walked in. The rule was simple: half the tables bookable in advance, half kept for drop-ins. At the first trial we placed a chalkboard by the door with three columns—Electrical, Fabric, Bikes—and sticky tags that visitors could move as they queued. A bell rang every 20 minutes to nudge progress, not hurry it. Bottlenecks shrank, but we noticed a problem: people with small fixes waited behind big jobs. We added a “triage” stool where a volunteer could tighten a screw or replace a plug without claiming a full slot. By the third month, the rota felt like a rhythm: green tags for booked slots, orange for drop-ins, and blue for the surge buffer. The tailor asked for a second lamp at her table; stitches went straighter. The café sold more tea because queues were shorter and calmer. Our last chart showed that average wait fell by a third without turning anyone away. We didn’t fix everything; we fixed the way of fixing.

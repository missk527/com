11+ Comprehension (CO8)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

When the museum announced a travelling exhibition called “Bones on the Move,” our class signed up for the Friday night sleepover. We packed torches, notebooks, and enough snacks to feed a small herd. After closing time, the museum educator, Kira, unlocked a side door and led us past a whale skeleton into a room full of crates. Each crate held pieces of prehistoric animals cushioned in foam: ribs like canoe paddles, teeth like pebbled knives, and a skull with one eye socket larger than the other. Kira said our job was to help set up labels for the public opening on Saturday. We didn’t touch the bones, of course. Gloves or not, that was for the trained staff. Instead, we checked the draft labels for clarity. Did a sentence explain the age of a fossil without three lines of numbers? Did the words “marine reptile” confuse readers who might expect a dinosaur? When we reached a tray of small bones, our classmate Jonah frowned. The label called them “left hind foot of Marsh Running Lizard,” but he noticed four ankle bones instead of three in the diagram on the poster behind us. Kira saw his hand go up and invited him to explain. Jonah showed that the diagram for Marsh Running Lizard—the exhibit’s star—always had three ankle bones in a specific pattern. The tray on the table had four, with a different shape at the heel. Kira nodded and asked us to compare with a book on the shelf. Ten minutes later we found a match: a smaller, related species that lived in the same region but used its feet differently for climbing riverbanks. Someone had mis-shelved the tray during packing. The correction changed more than a label. The climbing species’ story helped connect three sections of the exhibition: river fossils, trackways, and a case about how bone shapes reflect movement. We rewrote the sign to ask a question first—“What can ankles tell us about a life spent on slopes?”—then offered clues. If you looked closely, you could see the extra bone as a small bump, like a knot in a tree. We added a sketch so visitors wouldn’t need to guess. Near midnight, Kira spread sleeping mats between the crates. The museum felt different in the dark, quieter but also more awake, as if the bones were concentrating. Before lights out, she thanked us for our careful reading and curiosity. “Science is a conversation across time,” she said. “When you notice something, you join it.” In the morning, we watched families gather around the corrected display. Children pointed to the sketch and then to the tray, their fingers hovering respectfully above the foam. The sign didn’t claim we had discovered a new species. It showed how attention—plus a question—could reopen an old one and tell a clearer story.

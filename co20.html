11+ Comprehension (CO20)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Dress rehearsal night made the problem obvious: the new school theatre looked smart, but lines vanished in the back row. Our drama and physics clubs teamed up to tune the sound without buying expensive gear. We began with a clap test—short bursts of applause from different seats—to hear how long the echoes lasted. Then we borrowed a portable speaker and played a speech track while volunteers walked the aisles with clipboards, marking 'clear', 'muffled', or 'lost'. The hall’s shape—high ceiling, smooth walls—sent reflections bouncing like rubber balls. We built removable panels from wool batting wrapped in fire-safe fabric and hung them on temporary stands along the side walls. The idea wasn’t to make it dead silent; it was to tame the worst reflections so consonants could land. Our music teacher lent five floor screens, and the art department cut cardboard diffusers—zigzag shapes—to break up the bounce at the back wall. Testing took two evenings. With panels only, voices sounded closer but still smeared at the rear. With diffusers added, the clap tail shortened, and the back row heard the 't' and 'k' sounds more distinctly. We measured improvement crudely by counting how many boxes on the clipboards changed from 'lost' to 'clear'. The map of crosses turned patchy green. We also trained actors to turn slightly toward the middle on key lines and to pause a fraction after a joke so laughter wouldn’t swallow the next word. One snag remained: a low hum from the air system masked quiet speech. The caretaker showed us a hidden setting that reduced fan speed between scenes without shutting it off. We added a sign by the booth: 'Fan to Scene 2 during dialogue'. On final rehearsal, the difference was plain. The head teacher, who had missed earlier tests, sat in the back and repeated the sharpest line: “Steal my sandwich, will you?” It landed like a tossed coin, bright and definite. We wrote up a one-page guide for future shows: layout of panels and diffusers, where to stand mics if used, how to place floor screens for soloists, and stage directions for projecting without strain. We didn’t claim a perfect theatre. We left a kit in the storeroom labelled 'Acoustics—Open First', so the next cast wouldn’t have to start from guesswork. Good sound, we decided, is a promise you renew: measure, adjust, and listen together.

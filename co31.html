11+ Comprehension (CO31)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The footbridge over the river bounced when groups crossed it. Some people liked the springy feel; others worried. Our technology club asked the council for permission to test the bridge’s movements with small sensors. We used accelerometers stuck under the handrail and a phone app to log vertical motion in millimetres. The rule was to test only during daylight and to keep lanes clear. We ran three trials. First, we walked in a normal flow; second, we marched in step to match the bridge’s natural rhythm; third, we spread out in clusters. The readings showed small spikes for normal use, larger waves when we marched, and the smoothest pattern when people spaced themselves. A retired engineer told us that many footbridges can sway if crowds walk in sync, but spacing or adding damping stops problems. We built a poster with simple pictures: a stick figure walking, a group marching, and a spread-out parade. We suggested two changes: signs at each end—“Spread out when busy”—and rubber pads under the handrails where they touched the posts to absorb small vibrations. The council tested the pads and liked the difference. On market day, we counted fewer people marching in step after the signs appeared. The bridge still bounced a little; it now felt like a rhythm, not a wobble.

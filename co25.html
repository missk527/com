11+ Comprehension (CO25)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Our town runs a spring river clean-up each year. This time, our eco club added a data twist: count what we collected, not just bag it. We printed waterproof tally sheets with five categories—plastic bottles, cans, food wrappers, fishing line, and “other.” Teams walked different stretches with grabbers and buckets. We agreed on two rules: keep feet on paths near the bank, and leave anything that looked like a nest or a home alone. The first hour filled buckets fast. Wrappers blew into brambles like flags. We found five fishing lines tangled around a branch and cut them free after checking no nests were nearby. A parent brought a luggage scale so we could weigh full bags before adding them to the council’s skip. We also photographed any branded clusters to send to companies that sponsored the event. A sudden shower tested our waterproof sheets; the pencil marks held. Numbers shaped the afternoon. One bend near the picnic tables produced most cans. The underpass collected bottles after every rain. A quiet stretch by the reeds had almost nothing until we spotted a single tyre half sunk in the mud; we marked the location for the council’s grab truck. We added a column for glass when we noticed shards by a bench, then sent someone for a broom and a hard container so sharp pieces wouldn’t tear bags. At day’s end, we laid the sheets on a picnic table and tallied totals. We posted a bar chart and three requests: add covered bins near the picnic bend; place a net boom before the underpass during storms; and put a sign by the reeds asking people to report tyres instead of rolling them deeper. The mayor thanked volunteers and promised to try the bin change immediately. Our hands smelled of river and soap, and our map had fewer blank spots where litter had been hiding.

11+ Comprehension (CO40)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

A new night market brought colour and music to the riverbank—and bins that overflowed by ten. Our eco committee worked with stallholders to design a waste plan that would keep the path clear and the river clean. The rule was that no stall could hide bags behind it; all waste had to go to labelled points watched by volunteers. We began with a walk-through at setup. Food stalls received lidded tubs for compostables; drink stalls got stackable crates for returnable cups; and general waste points sat near exits with a bright sign: “Last chance bin.” We taped arrows on the ground leading to each point and drew a map for the steward briefing. A local canoe club offered to patrol the water for floating cups. The first weekend was messy. The wind flipped crate stacks and chased napkins. We added bungee cords and a “napkin net” — a lightweight frame with mesh that sat behind tables. A guitarist’s amplifier hid a waste sign; we raised signs on poles above head height. Volunteers gave out a “thank-you token” to any customer who returned a cup; tokens could be swapped for a discount on a stall of their choice at the end of the night. By the third market, the path looked passable even at closing. Canoeists reported fewer cups, and the riverbank flower beds were free of chip trays. Stallholders signed a rota: ten minutes before each music set, two of them walked the line with grabbers. We posted totals—bags by type and number of returned cups—on a chalkboard near the bus stop. The market still glittered; it no longer shed its shine into the river.

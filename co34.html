11+ Comprehension (CO34)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

The canteen used dried herbs for most dishes. Our gardening club wondered whether we could grow enough fresh flavour in a small patch by the kitchen door. We measured a rectangle barely three metres long and planned a staggered bed with thyme, parsley, chives, mint in tubs to contain it, and basil under a cold frame for cooler nights. The chef agreed to try any harvest if we brought it washed and labelled by Monday morning. We built the bed from reclaimed bricks and filled it with compost from the school heaps. A timetable put Year 6 on watering duty during hot weeks, with a simple rule: check soil first, then water to the roots. We added a rain gauge and a small sign asking students not to pick leaves before lunch. The first harvest was tiny—just a handful of chives and a sprig of thyme—but the potato soup tasted brighter and the chef wrote “garden” next to it on the menu board. By midterm, the bed grew thick. Mint reached for the path, so we lifted runners back into tubs. Basil sulked in a cold snap; the frame kept most leaves green. We dried a surplus of thyme on racks in the art room and crumbled it into jars for winter. Students voted for “herb butter day,” and the kitchen let them mix chopped parsley and chives into pats that melted on steamed carrots. We made a chart of which dishes changed with herbs—soups, pasta, roast chicken—and which didn’t—curry needed its spice blend, porridge remained plain. The head teacher asked us to write a one-sheet guide: which herb with which dish, how to wash and store, and how to avoid bitter stems. The patch didn’t feed the school, but it fed the idea that flavour can be grown a few steps from the pot.

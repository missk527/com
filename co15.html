11+ Comprehension (CO15)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

When the council planned to remove three old plane trees near the bus station, our geography class asked a different question: what do the rest of our town’s trees actually do for us? We proposed a weekend “Tree Inventory” to count street trees and estimate the shade, rain capture, and cooling they provide. The parks officer agreed if we shared our method publicly and didn’t climb anything. We divided the town map into small squares and assigned teams of two. Each pair carried a tape measure, a thermometer, and a phone with a simple survey: species (if known), trunk circumference at chest height, canopy width in steps, and pavement temperature in sun and shade. We learned to recognise a few common shapes—lollipop lindens, layered cherries, the broad hands of plane trees. In the square by the bakery, we recorded the hottest sun-on-tarmac temperature of the morning; in the square by the clinic, the shadiest bench stayed ten degrees cooler than the open pavement nearby. Back at school, we used a basic model to estimate leaf area from trunk size and matched it with published coefficients for rain intercepted during short storms. Our results were rough but revealing. A single mature plane tree could slow the fall of thousands of raindrops each week and cast shade across half the bus queue at noon. Streets with continuous canopies showed smoother temperature curves. A bare cul-de-sac near the new flats stayed hot until evening, like a frying pan that refused to cool. We made a one-page report for each square: a small map, three photos, and two short graphs—surface temperatures and estimated litres of first-flush rain held up by leaves. We posted them on the school website and invited comments. Some residents asked for more trees on their streets; others worried about roots and leaves in gutters. We recommended three actions: replace the bus-station trees one-for-one with disease-resistant varieties, add two small trees to the bare cul-de-sac, and schedule seasonal gutter clearing on the most leaf-heavy blocks. The council promised to review the plan and to walk two routes with us next month.

11+ Comprehension (CO37)

Time allowed: 30 minutes

Passage

Behind the sports hall, three tin sheds baked in summer and dripped in winter. Our eco team proposed fitting gutters and barrels to catch rain for the garden beds instead of dragging hoses across the car park. The caretaker agreed if we designed overflow paths so water would not flood the path or seep under the doors. We measured roof lengths and used a simple rule of thumb: 1 mm of rain on 1 square metre gives 1 litre. With chalk and string we marked gutter lines and chose two downpipes per shed into linked barrels. We added fine leaf guards and mosquito-proof mesh over the barrel lids. A maths group sized overflows to a gravel soakaway made from an old crate filled with stones. The first storm showed two flaws. Wind pushed rain past a warped roof edge on Shed B, and one barrel leaned on soft ground. We added a drip flashing strip along the edge and set the barrel on a level paving slab. We painted a clear mark on each barrel that read “Stop filling here—check overflow.” A sign above the tap showed pictures: watering can low, bucket high, hose not allowed. By the end of term, the garden team filled cans without trekked hoses. We logged volumes after big showers and found that the linked barrels filled fastest on Shed A facing the prevailing wind. The caretaker smiled at a dry threshold and asked for one more improvement: a winter drain bung so barrels could be emptied before frost. We wrote a one-page guide and left a spare mesh roll labelled “For leaves and summer midges.”
